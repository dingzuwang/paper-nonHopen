# Spectrum and stead-state properties of random Liouvillians

## Background

<!-- Lindbladians form  -->
The superoperator ${ \mathcal{L} }$ reads
$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathcal{L}
  &=
  \mathcal{L}[H] + \sum_{l} \mathcal{D} [W_{l}] \\
  &=
  - \mathrm{i} [H, \rho] + \sum_{l = 1}^{L} \left( W_{l} \rho W_{l}^{\dagger} - \frac{1}{2} W_{l}^{\dagger} W_{l}\rho - \frac{1}{2} \rho W_{l}^{\dagger} W_{l}  \right)
\end{align*}
$$
- unitary part: ${ \mathcal{L}[H] }$ 
- dissipative part: ${ \sum_{l} \mathcal{D}[W_{l}]  }$ 
- number of dissipative channels: ${ L }$ 
- jump operators: ${ W_{l} }$ 

<!-- Idea and some progresses -->

In the view of success of RMT, it's nature to ask if a similar approach can be followed for Lindbladians dynamics.

There are three works to follow this idea 

1. One studies an ensemble of random Lindblad operators with maximal number of channels, and acquires the universal lemon-shape form.
2. One studies an ensemble of random Lindbladians consisting of Hamiltonian part and finite number of Hermitian jump operators, and finds a sharp spectral transition versus dissipative strength.

<!-- Main point of the paper -->

The aim of this paper is to study the universal properties of the spectrum and the steady state of Liouvillian operators.

This Lindbladians consist of a Hamiltonian component and a set of dissipation channels, taken from appropriate ensembles of random matrices.

## Random Liouvillian ensembles

Defining a complete orthogonal basis set ${ \{G_{j}\} }$ for the space of operators
$$
\begin{align*}
  \operatorname{tr}\left[ G^{\dagger}_{i}G_{j} \right]
  &=
  \delta_{ij},\;\; i = 0,\cdots ,N^{2}- 1
\end{align*}
$$
- Hilbert space: ${ N }$ 
- the first basis: ${ G_{0} = \mathbb{I}/\sqrt{N} }$ 

Then each jump operator can be represented by such complete orthogonal basis
$$
\begin{align*}
  W_{l} 
  &=
  g\sum_{j = 1}^{N^{2}- 1} G_{j} w_{jl}
\end{align*}
$$
- coefficients vector about ${ j }$-th jump operator: ${ w_{jl} }$ 
- dimension of ${ w_{jl} }$
$$
\begin{align*}
  N^{2}- 1 \times r
\end{align*}
$$
- number of jump operator: ${ r }$ 

Due to ${ G_{0} = 0 }$ and it's orthogonal with Identity, ${ W_{l} }$ is traceless, which ensure no contribution to Hamiltonian part.



Thus we can get the dissipative term as
$$
\begin{align*}
  \sum_{l = 1}^{r} W_{l}\rho W^{\dagger}_{l}
  &=
  g^{2} \sum_{j,k = 1}^{N^{2}-1} \sum_{l} w_{jl}w^*_{kl} G_{j} \hat{\rho} G^{\dagger}_{k} 
  =
  g^{2}\sum_{j,k = 1}^{N^{2}- 1}(ww^{\dagger})_{jk}G_{j}\rho G^{\dagger}_{k} \\
  &\equiv
  g^{2} \sum_{j,k = 1}^{N^{2}-1}  d_{jk}G_{j}\rho G^{\dagger}_{k} \\
  \sum_{l = 1}^{r} W^{\dagger}_{l}W_{l}\rho 
  &=
  g^{2} \sum_{j,k = 1}^{N^{2} - 1}  \sum_{l = 1}^{r} w^*_{kl}w_{jl} G^{\dagger}_{k}G_{j}\rho
  =
  g^{2} \sum_{j,k = 1}^{N^{2} - 1} (ww^{\dagger})_{jk}G^{\dagger}_{k}G_{j}\rho\\
  &=
  g^{2}\sum_{j,k = 1}^{N^{2}-1} d_{jk}G^{\dagger}_{k}G_{j}\rho\\
  \sum_{l = 1}^{r} \rho W_{l}^{\dagger} W_{l}
  &=
  g^{2}\sum_{j,k = 1}^{N^{2} - 1}  \sum_{l = 1}^{r} w^*_{kl} w_{jl} \rho G_{k}^{\dagger} G_{j} \\
  &=
  g^{2} \sum_{j,k = 1}^{N^{2} - 1} (ww^{\dagger})_{jk} \rho G_{k}^{\dagger} G_{j}
  =
  g^{2} \sum_{j,k = 1}^{N^{2}-1}  d_{jk}\rho G_{k}^{\dagger} G_{j}
\end{align*}
$$

Finally we get the random Liouvillian equation
$$
\begin{align*}
  - \mathrm{i}[H, \rho] + g^{2}\left[\sum_{j,k = 1}^{N^{2}-1} d_{jk} \left( G_{j}\rho G^{\dagger}_{k} - \frac{1}{2} G^{\dagger}_{k}G_{j}\rho - \frac{1}{2} \rho G^{\dagger}_{k}G_{j} \right) \right]
\end{align*}
$$
Positive-defined matrix ${ d_{jk} }$ with dimension ${ (N^{2}- 1) \times (N^{2}- 1) }$.

To obtain a random Liouvillian, we draw ${ H }$ from Gaussian ensemble and ${ w }$ from the Ginibre ensemble.

Consider two case
1. ${ \beta = 1 }$ real matrices: ${ H_{ij} = H_{ji} }$ and ${ w_{ij} \in \mathbb{R} }$ 
2. ${ \beta = 2 }$ complex matrices: ${ H_{ij} = H_{ji}^*  }$ and ${ w_{ij} \in \mathbb{C} }$ 


## Spectral properties

This random Liouvillians are parameterize by ${ N, r, \beta, g }$.

The variance along the real axis read
$$
\begin{align*}
  X^{2}
  &=
  \sum_{\alpha = 0}^{N^{2}- 1} \frac{[\Re(\Lambda_{\alpha} - R)]^{2}}{N^{2}}
\end{align*}
$$
- eigenvalue: ${ \Lambda_{\alpha} }$ 
- number of eigenvalue: ${ N^{2} }$ 
- center of mass of the spectrum
$$
\begin{align*}
  R = \sum_{\alpha} \frac{\Lambda_{\alpha}}{N^{2}}
\end{align*}
$$

The variance of the imaginary axis reads
$$
\begin{align*}
  Y^{2}
  &=
  \sum_{\alpha = 0}^{N^{2}-1}  \frac{[\Im(\Lambda_{\alpha})]^{2}}{N^{2}}
\end{align*}
$$

**Results**

1. As the dissipation strength increase, the boundary form evolves from ellipse to lemon-like shape.
2. For strong dissipation regime, ${ r = 2 }$ is enough to indicate the lemon-list shape.




## Generate Basis
For the basis only have off-diagonal, we can obtain them by
$$
\begin{align*}
  H_{ij}^{+} 
  &=
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (E_{ij} + E_{ji}) \\
  H_{ij}^{ -}
  &=
  \frac{i}{\sqrt{2}} (E_{ij} - E_{ji}) \\
\end{align*}
$$
where ${ E_{ij} }$ mean a matrix with element ${ E_{ij}= \delta_{ij} }$ and ${ j>i }$. 

Number of these basis is ${ N(N - 1) }$.

For the basis only have diagonal, we can obtain them by 
$$
\begin{align*}
  H_{ii}
  &=
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{k(k + 1)}} \text{diag} (1 \cdots 1, - k, 0\cdots 0),\;\; k = 1,\cdots ,N - 1
\end{align*}
$$

With a normlized identity
$$
\begin{align*}
  H_{0}
  &=
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \mathbb{I}
\end{align*}
$$
