# Complex spacing ratio: a signature of dissipative quantum chaos


## Ration

Assuming set ${ \{\lambda_{k}\}_{k = 1}^{N} }$ is the spectrum of a Hermitian or non-Hermitian matrix. The spacing ratio define as
$$
\begin{align*}
  z_{k}
  &=
  \frac{\lambda_{k}^{\text{NN}} - \lambda_{k}}{\lambda_{k}^{\text{NNN}} - \lambda_{k}}
\end{align*}
$$
- ${ k }$-th eigenvalue: ${ \lambda_{k} }$ 
- nearest-neighbor distance eigenvalue: ${ \lambda_{k}^{\text{NN}} }$
- next-nearest-neighbor distance eigenvalue: ${ \lambda_{k}^{\text{NNN}} }$


Therefore we can obtain
$$
\begin{align*}
  z_{k}
  &:=
  \frac{(re^{i\theta})_\text{NN} }{(r e^{i\theta})_\text{NNN} }
  = 
  r e^{i\theta}
\end{align*}
$$
- distance ratio for real levels: ${ z \equiv r \in [ -1, 1] }$ 
- distance ratio for complex levels: ${ r \in [0, 1] }$

**Conclusion**
1. One expects integrable model to have the same ratio statistics as uncorrelated levels, and chaotic model to follow Ginibre statistics.
2. For uncorrelated levels, reference level don't influence its two nearest levels, so all spacing ratio ${ z }$ have the same probability, which yield a flat distribution in complex space.
3. For random matrices, one expects level repulsion, so the ration density should vanish at the origin and suppression at small angles.

## Probability distribution

For the uncorrelated levels, due to the flat ratio density, its probability distribution is uniform in the circle
$$
\begin{align*}
  \varrho_\text{Poi} (z)
  &=
  \frac{1}{\pi} \Theta( 1 - |z|)
\end{align*}
$$
- step function: 
$$
\begin{align*}
  \Theta(1 - |z|) = 1\;(|z|<0);\;\; 0\;(|z|>0)
\end{align*}
$$

Radial marginal distribution
$$
\begin{align*}
  \int_{0}^{1} \varrho_\text{Poi}(r)  \mathrm{d}r \, = 1
  \;\; 
  \to 
  \;\;  
  \varrho_\text{Poi}(r) 
  =
  \int_{0}^{2\pi} \frac{1}{\pi} r \mathrm{d}\theta \, 
  =
  2r
\end{align*}
$$

Angular marginal distribution
$$
\begin{align*}
  \int_{0}^{2\pi} \varrho_\text{Poi}(\theta)  \mathrm{d}\theta \, = 1
  \;\; 
  \to 
  \;\; 
  \varrho _\text{Poi} (\theta) 
  =
  \int_{0}^{1} \frac{1}{\pi}  r \mathrm{d}r \, 
  =
  \frac{1}{2\pi} 
\end{align*}
$$

*Math*
$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathrm{d}A
  &=
  \frac{1}{2} (r + \mathrm{d}r)^{2} \mathrm{d}\theta - \frac{1}{2} r^{2}\mathrm{d}\theta
  =
  \frac{1}{2} (r^{2} + 2r\mathrm{d}r + (\mathrm{d}r)^{2}- r^{2}) \mathrm{d}\theta \\
  &=
  r\mathrm{d}r\mathrm{d}\theta + \frac{1}{2} (dr)^{2}\mathrm{d}\theta \\
  &\simeq
  r \mathrm{d}r \mathrm{d}\theta
\end{align*}
$$

Therefore, the area can be written as 
$$
\begin{align*}
  \int_{0}^{2\pi} \int_{0}^{1} \frac{1}{\pi} \cdot r\mathrm{d}r \, \mathrm{d}\theta \, 
  &=
  1
\end{align*}
$$

## Boundary-driven spin chain

Lindblad master equation reads
$$
\begin{align*}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\rho(t)
  &=
  - i[H, \rho(t)] + \sum_{\mu= 1}^{D} \left( W_{\mu} \rho(t) W^{\dagger}_{\mu} - \frac{1}{2} \left\lbrace W^{\dagger}_{\mu} W_{\mu}, \rho(t) \right\rbrace\right)
\end{align*}
$$

Hamiltonian reads
$$
\begin{align*}
  H 
  =&
  J\sum_{l = 1}^{N - 1} (\sigma_{l}^{x}\sigma_{l + 1}^{x} + \sigma_{l}^{y}\sigma_{l+1}^{y} + \Delta \sigma_{l}^{z}\sigma_{l + 1}^{z}) + \\
  &
  J'\sum_{l = 1}^{N - 2} (\sigma_{l}^{x}\sigma_{l + 2}^{x} + \sigma_{l}^{y}\sigma_{l+ 2}^{y} + \Delta' \sigma_{l}^{z}\sigma_{l + 2}^{z})
\end{align*}
$$

Bulk dephasing of all spin
$$
\begin{align*}
  W_{\mu} = \sqrt{\gamma} \sigma^{z}_{\mu},\;\; \mu \in \{1,\cdots ,N\}
\end{align*}
$$
Amplitude dephasing at boundaries
$$
\begin{align*}
  W_{N + 1} &= \sqrt{\gamma_{L}^{+}}\sigma_{1}^{ +},\;\; W_{N + 2} = \sqrt{\gamma_{L}^{ -}}\sigma_{1}^{ -} \\
  W_{N + 3} &= \sqrt{\gamma_{R}^{ +}}\sigma_{N}^{ +},\;\;  W_{N + 4} = \sqrt{\gamma_{R}^{ -}}\sigma_{N}^{ -}
\end{align*}
$$
- dephasing rate: ${ \gamma }$ 
- spin injection strength: ${ \gamma_{L}^{ +}, \gamma_{R}^{ +} }$ 
- spin extraction strength: ${ \gamma_{L}^{ -}, \gamma_{R}^{ -} }$ 

**The model is characterized by ${ J, J', \Delta, \Delta', \gamma, \gamma_{L}^{ +}, \gamma_{R}^{ +}, \gamma_{L}^{ -}, \gamma_{R}^{ -} }$ which allows us to tune its integrability or chaotic.**

In the Liouville space ${ \mathcal{K} }$, the Lindblad superoperator reads
$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathcal{L}
  =&
  - \mathrm{i} \left( H \otimes \mathbb{I} - \mathbb{I} \otimes H^{T} \right) + \\
  &
  \quad \left( \sum_{\mu = 1}^{D} W_{\mu}  \otimes W^*_{\mu} - \frac{1}{2} W^{\dagger}_{\mu}W_{\mu}\otimes \mathbb{I} - \mathbb{I} \otimes \left( \frac{1}{2} W_{\mu}^{\dagger}W_{\mu} \right)^{T} \right) \\
  =&
  - \mathrm{i} \left[ \left( H - \frac{\mathrm{i}}{2}\sum_{\mu}^{}  W^{\dagger}_{\mu}W_{\mu} \right) \otimes \mathbb{I} - \mathbb{I} \otimes \left( H + \frac{\mathrm{i}}{2} \sum_{\mu} W^{\dagger}_{\mu}W_{\mu} \right)^{T} \right] +\\
  &
  \quad \sum_{\mu = 1}^{D} W_{\mu} \otimes W^*_{\mu}
\end{align*}
$$

The superoperator ${ \mathcal{S}^{z} }$, total ${ z }$-axis magnetization ${ S^{z} = \sum_{l = 1}^{N} \sigma_{l}^{z} }$, reads
$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathcal{S}^{z}
  &=
  S^{z} \otimes \mathbb{I} - \mathbb{I} \otimes {S^{z}}^{T}
\end{align*}
$$

Due to 
$$
\begin{align*}
  [\mathcal{L}, \mathcal{S}^{z}] = 0
\end{align*}
$$

We can define a conserved quantum number ${ M }$
$$
\begin{align*}
  M = \sum_{l = 1}^{2N} \frac{1 + s_{l}}{2}  
\end{align*}
$$
- spin configuration: ${ s_{l} = \pm 1 }$ 

${ M }$ is the number of spin up in the larger Hilbert space ${ S^{z}\otimes \mathbb{I} + \mathbb{I} \otimes S^{z} }$
$$
\begin{align*}
  2M = \sum_{l = 1}^{2N} s_{l} + 2N
\end{align*}
$$

In order to make the ${ M }$ still satisfy that ''number of spin up in larger Hilbert space'', we rewrite the total magnetization in the Liouville space as
$$
\begin{align*}
 2M =  \sum_{l = 1}^{N} (s_{l} - s'_{l}) + 2N
\end{align*}
$$

**Five case**
1. Boundary-driven XX chain with bulk dephasing. It's Bethe-ansatz integrable.
$$
\begin{align*}
  J = 1, \Delta = J' = \Delta' = 0, \gamma = 1, \gamma_{L}^{ +} = 0.5, \gamma_{L}^{ -} = 1.2, \gamma_{R}^{ + } = 1.0, \gamma_{R}^{ -} = 0.8
\end{align*}
$$

2. Heisenberg chain with pure-source or pure-sink without bulk dephasing. Its steady state is integrable but the bulk of spectrum is likely not.
$$
\begin{align*}
  J = \Delta = 1, J'= \Delta'= 0, \gamma = 0, \gamma_{L}^{+ } = 0.6, \gamma_{L}^{ -} = 0, \gamma_{R}^{ +} = 0, \gamma_{R}^{ -} = 1.4
\end{align*}
$$

3. *XXX* chain with arbitrary boundary driving without bulk dephasing. It's not integrable even for steady state.
$$
\begin{align*}
  J = \Delta = 1, J'= \Delta'= 0, \gamma = 0, \gamma_{L}^{ +} = 0.5, \gamma_{L}^{ -} = 0.3, \gamma_{R}^{ +} = 0.3, \gamma_{R}^{ -} = 0.9
\end{align*}
$$

4. *XXZ* chain with next-to-nearest-neighbors interactions with arbitrary boundary driving without bulk dephasing. It's not integrable.
$$
\begin{align*}
  J = 1, \Delta = 0.3, J'= 1, \Delta'= 1.5, \gamma = 0, \gamma_{L}^{ +} = 0.5, \gamma_{L}^{ -} = 0.3, \gamma_{R}^{ +} = 0.3, \gamma_{R}^{ -} = 0.9
\end{align*}
$$

5. Random Liouvillian at strong dissipation.
$$
\begin{align*}
  N = 80, \beta = 2, r = 2, g = 100
\end{align*}
$$

**Results**
1. Dephasing-*XX* model display the similar behavior with uncorrelated levels.
2. Heisenberg chain with integrable steady state shows an intermediate behavior between Poisson and RMT statistics.
3. Other systems clearly conform the RMT statistics.
4. These results indicate that complex spacing ratios indeed offer a signature of quantum chaos in Markovian setups.

## Single-number signature

*Aiming to capture the main feature of distribution of complex spacing ratio through a reduced set of numbers, here introducing single-number signature.*

**Degree of level repulsion**

The degree of level repulsion reads
$$
\begin{align*}
  \alpha = \lim_{r \to 0} \log \varrho(r)/\log r
\end{align*}
$$
- radial marginal distribution: ${ \varrho(r) }$ 

It describes the power-law behavior of ${ \varrho(r) }$ as ${ r \to 0 }$.

1. ${ \alpha = \beta }$, Hermitian random matrix
2. ${ \alpha = 3 }$, non-Hermitian random matrix
3. ${ \alpha = 0 }$, real uncorrelated levels
4. ${ \alpha = 1 }$, complex uncorrelated levels

Actual computation of ${ \alpha }$ for a given spectrum introduces a large relative error.

## Disorder non-Hermitian Hamiltonian

*Spectrum property in many-body localized regime resemble those of integrable model.*

Hamiltonian reads
$$
\begin{align*}
  H 
  =&
  \sum_{j = 1}^{N} - J (b_{j}^{\dagger}b_{j + 1} + b_{j}b_{j + 1}^{\dagger}) + U n_{j}n_{j + 1} \\
  &+
  \sum_{j = 1}^{N} \left( h_{j} + \mathrm{i} ( - 1)^{j} \gamma \right) n_{j}
\end{align*}
$$
- hopping strength: ${ J }$ 
- short-range repulsion: ${ U }$ 
- non-Hermiticity: ${ \gamma }$ 
- uniform disorder distribution: ${ h_{j} \in [ - h, h] }$ 
- particle-number operator: ${ n_{j} }$ 

The on-site gain/loss term is ${ (h_{j} + \mathrm{i}( - 1)^{j}\gamma)n_{j} }$ arising the non-Hermiticity.

**parameter**

${ J = 1, U = 2, \gamma = 0.1 }$ (weak non-Hermiticity)
1. delocalized regime: ${ h = 2 }$ 
2. localized regime: ${ h = 10 }$ 

**results**
1. Delocalized regime supports Poisson statistics (flat distribution), and localized regime conforms to RMT statistics.
2. From single-number signature, the finite-size scaling is consistent with uncorrelated levels in localized regime.
3. But in the delocalized regime, it dose not conform to GinUE and attains to ${ AI^{\dagger}  }$ from a symmetry class.

*Complex ratio can also be used to distinguish between random matrix ensembles with different symmetries.*
